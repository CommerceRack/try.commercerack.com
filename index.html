SMS vs. App Push

It is very common for app push to be confused with short message service (SMS). SMS or "Texting" is a receiver pays per message service offered by most cellular services which is limited to around 150 characters per message. App Push is sender pays reliable transport offered by mobile platforms such as Android (GCM), Apple IOS (APNS), and Amazon Kindle (ADM). Unlike SMS which is sent to a telephone number, an App push is sent directly to an application subscriber id that was created when the app was installed. The size and capabilities of app push vary by operating system, but since messages are actually sent to the receiving application it can include instructions to download any amount of content. SMS can contain multimedia audio or video as attachments only, whereas App Push is only limited by the device and could even contain two way video, audio, location or more.

EMAIL vs. App Push

There are several well known issues businesses face when communicating with email which demonstrates why app-push could eventually replace email.

  • Spam Filters: the original design of email has no way to authenticate legitimacy of senders, so complicated filters effectively "guess" the nature of messages. With app push the consent is implicit with the install of the application and so no such filters exist.
  • Email Formatting: email was originally designed for text on dumb terminals, and pre-dates html/web by almost 20 years. due to some early design limitations and incompatibilities it is not possible to use modern html5, css3, and javascript. It is generally understood with email it is possible to embed graphics, but it is generally not possible to leverage the easier and more powerful features of html5 and css. App-push is not subject to these limitations, it can display both email messages and also the enhanced messages.
  • List Maintenance: due to incompatibilities in email/smtp clients there is not a reliable way to detect if a message was delivered. ESP's require email marketers to perform regular list maintenance and expire emails that have not been confirmed. App-push does not have these restrictions, as long as the app is installed on the device it will be able to receive notifications (unless the device owner explicitly disallows notifications for the app).

Elastic Search

CommerceRack relies on ElasticSearch a robust tunable open source search engine. ElasticSearch provides flexible ways to query data, and is automatically updated typically within a few seconds of changes. ElasticSearch objects can index custom properties as well making it rather straightforward to extend the fields being indexed/searched as necessary.

Layered Search using Elastic

Layered Search lets shoppers filter by multiple attributes on a search is rapidly becoming the defacto way to find products. Virtually all e-commerce solutions require expensive third party integrations to offer this capability. CommerceRack is compatible with those services as well, but in most cases they are not required since ElasticSearch can provide the same functionality.

  • PRIVATE: contains administrative data including orders, crm tickets and customer data.

Company History

CommerceRack and anyCommerce started out as concepts in 2009 by the developers of CommerceRack, a privately held small business e-commerce website hosting and marketplace management company with 13 years of expertise in e-commerce. CommerceRack is a cloud service infrastructure that enables transactional commerce. CommerceRack utilizes anyCommerce's open-source framework to create unified experiences for e-commerce, m-commerce, social commerce and retail/brick and mortar businesses.

By 2011, most browsers on the market were releasing versions which could run the anyCommerce technology, and cloud services were rapidly growing in both popularity and stability. Recognizing the size and scope of this opportunity led to a decision by CommerceRack's management team to spend six months focusing on a prototype which was completed in early 2012.

After numerous unforeseen delays and failed light-bulb experiments, the decision to stop boarding new clients and focus all energies toward developing exclusively for the cloud was made as it proved to be the best way to pursue the company's long term revenue goals. An end of life sunset date for SAAS hosted sites was announced and all resources were channeled into cloud products. The CommerceRack system was migrated to the new anyCommerce technology and the CommerceRack project was unveiled in Q1 of 2014.

Getting Started with CommerceRack

The Cloud includes a lot of choices, in most cases if the right choice isn't immediately obvious, then it's probably safe to assume all the choices are approximately the same.

  1. Select Instance/License Type: Public Cloud, Private Cloud, or SAAS.
    When comparing offerings across providers it is important to understand the differences between DEDICATED, VIRTUALIZED, or MULTI-TENANT/SAAS instances.
    • DEDICATED instances have a committed input/output committed rate that will never be exceeded, or reduced, but resources are billed even when they are not in-use.
    • VIRTUALIZED instances will have a committed input/output rate which will be lower, but also allow bursting when available and will be more affordable than dedicated resources, although bursting may not be available when it is necessary.
    • MULTI-TENANT/SAAS (Software as a Service) is least expensive form because all resources are shared between tenants. SAAS is managed by one company, effectively acting as a 'property management' company for all the tenants. SAAS is a great way to save money for small companies, but configuration options are typically limited.
    Other considerations:
    • Dedicated, Virtualized, or SAAS can be deployed within public or private cloud. (In the case of SAAS the provider chooses the infrastructure)
    • Public Clouds offer a limited number of fixed configurations with varying memory, disk and performance configurations.
    • Public clouds are available from Amazon, Google, and other companies using OpenStack and can be either dedicated or virtualized.
    • Public clouds will always require companies to have their own independent primary care.
    • Private clouds allow companies to tweak the configurations of their own dedicated cloud instances, and frequently include some level of primary care with the service.
    • SAAS installations are deployed by Primary Care providers who will utilize either a public and private cloud infrastructure. SAAS configurations are always bundled with support and infrastructure, because the infrastructure is the responsibility of the SAAS provider. SAAS pricing is often transaction based, and is best suited for startup or companies who need an easy way to get started.
    • SAAS installations may also include specialized templates, or additional industry-specific capabilities or plugins that are not part of the general CommerceRack service and only available from that vendor.
    • WITH EITHER virtualized or dedicated instances the business has complete control about when and if upgrades to the software are performed.
    • As you might have expected choosing multi-tenant SAAS, virtualized or dedicated has an impact on support options for commerceRack. SAAS provider will always provide their own CommerceRack Support.
    • VIRTUALIZED or DEDICATED providers may provide their own support, or resell commercerack support.
  2. Select Instance+License: The License Type will determine which infrastructure providers will be appropriate, and the costs.
    When purchased separately the licensing costs for commerceRack range are based on the size/cost of the instance. Developers who plan to offer SAAS deployments can obtain free licenses. Software licensing does not include support, although some private/SAAS companies will offer their own support, which we recommend whenever it is available.
    Required Services:
    • Instances, also called computing units which roughly translates to server capacity.
    • Network Disk Storage - which usually measured by performance and gigabytes
    • Bandwidth - which is usually measured by gigabytes transferred, and License Fees - which grant permission to run software on an instance, and will often vary based on instance size.
    In addition there are optional services including:
    • Managed Database Hosting, Hot Object Cache, Dedicated IO Channels, and Content Distribution Networks. optional services perform specific tasks much more efficiently and can further boost performance and usually reduce operating expenses.
    • Now optional services don’t necessarily equate to higher costs or additional fees, quite to the contrary. For example specialized cache services such as memcache or redis can dramatically boost performance on instances and can in some cases do the same work for less than (0.01%) point zero one percent of the price of doing the same work on a general instance.
    Identifying when these specialized services can be used, then deploying, tuning and monitoring these is the role of a service provider for the infrastructure.
  3. Select a Service Provider: Companies which lack in-house skills will want to retain one or more companies with technical expertise to assist in deployment, and ongoing maintenance.
    For companies who select a cloud provider which does not offer support with infrastructure it must be purchased separately on either pre-paid or monthly basis. Technical support is not required, but is recommended for businesses who want to ensure their system is operating at peak efficiency. Regardless of company size - the biggest expense of any cloud infrastructure will be the primary care provider who will act as a general contractor to BE ACCOUNTABLE for the overall health. Primary care providers can be in-house technical staff, BUT ARE MORE commonly outside companies with personnel which are available to answer questions handle emergencies at all times. A company's primary care provider will be responsible for a variety of tasks such as setup, monitoring, tear-down, backups, upgrades, and other general maintenance. CommerceRack support packages are available at a variety of price points ranging from free forum support, to several thousand dollars a month for committed service levels. Self sign-up options for non-SAAS configurations are available online or over the phone. CommerceRack Support includes specialized know-how including: backend diagnostics, configuration, and performance monitoring application development/debugging assistance marketplace specialists for eBay, Amazon, Rakutan, Newegg and others and certifications to access the API’s for companies like eBay, Amazon, UPS, FedEx and others. Primary care providers will frequently offer additional services such as graphic design, search engine marketing, or development, or provide referrals to other companies. As with any type of project both fixed rate and hourly prices vary based on the types of service, length of service, and level of expertise involved. For those on a budget services like elance, and odesk can provide competent offshore resources for as little as a few dollars per hour. But some companies may find managing those resources, along with difficulties communicating requirements and related delays may be more expensive and frustrating than hiring an experienced onshore firm.

Because CommerceRack uses apps which will usually result in 1/10th the utilization of resources that a normal web application does -- a free tier is routinely sufficient for many small companies doing less than $1m in sales per year.
